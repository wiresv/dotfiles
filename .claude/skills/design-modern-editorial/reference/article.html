Essay · n° xxiii on writing slowly

The case for the first draft.

Why the worst version of a piece is, almost always, the most useful one — and what we lose when we polish too early.

By M. Aguila · 1,840 words 28 April 2026

Every writer I admire has, at some point in their career, made the same small public confession: the first draft is terrible, and that is the entire point. It is supposed to be terrible. The page that emerges from a clean morning of writing is not a draft so much as a survey — a quick rough map of where the territory might be. The polish that makes a piece feel inevitable comes later, in the slow second pass, where the writer learns what the draft was trying to be all along.

The trouble with the contemporary tools of writing is that they are very good at hiding this stage from us. Word processors auto-correct. Editors suggest. Models1 rewrite the awkward sentence into something fluent before we have had time to decide whether the awkwardness was the point. The first draft, in other words, has been quietly automated out of existence.

i. The honest mess What a first draft is actually for

A first draft is a thinking instrument before it is a piece of writing. Its job is not to communicate to a reader; its job is to clarify, to the writer, what the writer believes. The reason it reads badly is that it is doing two things at once — making a thought and recording it — and neither task gets the writer's full attention. This is fine. It is, in fact, the only honest way to begin.

"The page is a place to find out what you think — not to perform what you already know." — a teacher, somewhere, every year of school

The teacher quoted above is apocryphal. I have heard it attributed to Didion2, to Forster3, to half a dozen others — and the attribution rarely matters, because the sentiment is correct independent of who first said it. Writing is a way of thinking that leaves a record. A first draft is the record before it has been edited into something a reader can use.

ii. The case for friction On the use of being slow

There is a small genre of essays — I am, of course, contributing to it now — that argues for the value of writing by hand, on paper, in pen. The reasoning is usually some mix of nostalgia and neurology. I do not particularly trust the neurology, but I am sympathetic to the nostalgia. What pen and paper offer, more than anything else, is friction: a writing process that is expensive enough to require a thought before each sentence.

A first draft on a screen is too cheap. The cursor blinks; the sentence arrives; the sentence is replaced. We can write three opening paragraphs in the time it would take, on paper, to commit to one. The result is a kind of high-throughput, low-conviction prose — fluent, generic, and very hard to remember.

iii. A defence of the awkward sentence or, when not to fix it

The clearest test of whether a sentence belongs in a first draft is whether you remember writing it the next morning. The fluent ones — the ones that seemed to write themselves — almost never pass. The awkward ones, the ones you wrote because you could not think of how else to say it, almost always do. They are the points where the draft ran into the actual difficulty of the subject. The fluent ones are the points where it didn't.

Edit toward the awkward sentences. They are where the piece is. Polish everything else into invisibility around them.
